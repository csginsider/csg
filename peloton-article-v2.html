A Confession About January

Every year, around January 2nd, something predictable happens. Gym parking lots fill up. Running shoe sales spike. Peloton's website gets a traffic surge. The resolution industrial complex grinds into motion, and for a few weeks, the whole country pretends it's a different kind of person.

By Valentine's Day, about half of those people have quietly stood down. By March, the evidence shows up on Facebook Marketplace: lightly used Pelotons, often still in the original packaging area of a spare bedroom, listed for $600–$800. The seller paid $2,500.

I bought one of those bikes. My wife and I ride it almost every day. We pay Peloton $44 a month for the subscription. We have never paid Peloton a dollar for the hardware.

What I didn't know when I bought it — and what most people covering Peloton still don't fully understand — is that we are exactly the kind of customer that confused Peloton's business model and then quietly saved it. Three years of shareholder letters document the realization in real time, one quarter at a time. The resolution quitter who lists their bike in March turns out to be one of the most valuable things that ever happened to this company.

Section 01

How the Resolution Cycle Built — and Broke — Peloton

Peloton's pandemic boom wasn't really a fitness story. It was a captive audience story. When gyms closed in March 2020 and stayed closed through most of 2021, the January resolution crowd had nowhere to go. A company that had been growing steadily at $915M in FY2020 revenue suddenly found itself with a waiting list. Revenue hit $4 billion. The stock hit $171. Management started building a factory in Ohio.

Then gyms reopened. The January resolution crowd remembered why they liked leaving the house. The bike went from the center of the living room to the spare bedroom to Facebook Marketplace, and Peloton's demand evaporated with a speed that left the company holding $1.4 billion in inventory it couldn't sell.

Total revenue fell from $3.58 billion in FY2022 to $2.44 billion by FY2025. The factory in Ohio became a write-off. Three restructuring plans in three years. Headcount from roughly 9,000 to under 3,000. The stock went from $171 to the single digits.

That's the story everyone knows. What almost nobody wrote about is the line in the income statement that barely moved through all of it.

Hardware revenue fell 60% — from $2.15 billion to $858 million. Subscription revenue went from $1.43 billion to $1.58 billion. That's right: through the demand collapse, through the restructuring plans, through the CEO exits, subscription revenue actually grew by $149 million.

The people who bought Pelotons during the pandemic and kept riding them were still paying $44 a month. That base held. And it held because Peloton had something the resolution crowd discovered even when they stopped using it in February: a product that worked well enough that the people who stuck with it genuinely didn't want to stop.

The quitters funded the hardware boom. The people who didn't quit funded the recovery. And then, quietly, the quitters came back — not as new hardware customers, but as used equipment activations. The financial statements document all three chapters.

"The people who quit Peloton in February were exactly who Peloton needed in March — they just needed to become sellers, not subscribers."

Section 02

The Margin Story: What the Resolution Cycle Cost Them

Here's what the resolution boom actually did to Peloton's economics, told through the gross margin lines. In FY2022 — still riding pandemic momentum but beginning to unwind — hardware gross margin was negative 27%. Every $2,500 bike Peloton shipped that year cost them roughly $3,175 to build and deliver. The January rush was real. The economics were not.

That negative hardware margin was partly a manufacturing immaturity story, partly a logistics crisis story, and partly the consequence of building supply chain capacity to serve a demand level that turned out to be temporary. When the resolution crowd went back to their gyms, Peloton was left holding costs built for a larger business than the one that remained.

The subscription business sat through all of this at 67–70% gross margin — untouched, structurally intact, quietly generating the cash that funded the restructuring. The instructor-led classes cost roughly the same to stream to 2.8 million subscribers as they did to 3.1 million. Fixed cost leverage is the reason subscription businesses survive demand shocks better than hardware businesses.

Section 03

Fewer Subscribers, More Revenue: The Quitter Math

Here's where the resolution cycle connects directly to the financials. Paid connected fitness subscriptions peaked at 3.08 million in FY2023 — the trailing echo of the pandemic boom, as the last wave of resolution buyers activated their bikes and started paying monthly. By FY2025 that number had fallen to 2.80 million, a 9% decline from peak.

And yet subscription revenue went from $1.57 billion to $1.58 billion over the same period. Fewer subscribers, more subscription revenue. The math works because the people who stayed are paying more — and because the secondary market is quietly adding a new kind of subscriber that pays the same monthly fee but cost nothing to acquire on the hardware side.

The secondary market subscriber is the key character in the ARPU story. They buy a used bike from someone who quit in March — paying nothing to Peloton for the hardware — then subscribe at the same $44/month as everyone else. From Peloton's subscription economics perspective, they are indistinguishable from someone who paid $2,500 for a new bike. And critically, the filings say their churn is lower than rental subscribers.

The behavioral logic makes sense. Someone who bought a used Peloton for $600 on Facebook Marketplace did so deliberately. They didn't get swept up in a New Year's resolution campaign. They researched it, sought it out, and paid a price that doesn't carry the psychological weight of a $2,500 commitment. There's no sunk cost hanging over them. They either ride it or they don't — and the data says they mostly ride it.

Every secondary market subscriber is pure subscription revenue. Zero hardware margin, zero hardware acquisition cost. The January quitter funded the original sale. The secondary buyer funds the ongoing subscription. Both pay $44 a month. Only one cost Peloton anything to acquire.

Section 04

How the Filings Documented the Pivot — Quarter by Quarter

The most striking thing about this story is that Peloton disclosed every step of the strategic shift as it happened. Reading the quarterly shareholder letters in sequence, you can watch management go from treating secondary market buyers as a leakage problem to building an entire marketplace to capture the channel. The language evolves precisely with the strategy, one filing at a time.

The contrast between the shareholder letter and the earnings call in August 2024 is worth sitting with. The letter says "onboarding experience." The CEO says "incremental revenue and gross profit." Those two framings aren't contradictory — both are true — but they prioritize very different things. The filings tell you the experience framing. The earnings call tells you the economics framing. Reading both is the whole picture.

The Economics of Repowered

On Repowered, Peloton earns 30% of every used equipment sale — on hardware it has already sold once, already earned revenue on, and no longer carries on its balance sheet. The seller gets 70%. The buyer gets a $45 activation fee instead of $95.

The incentive structure is explicit: route your used Peloton sale through Peloton's platform and you pay half the activation fee at subscription. That $50 discount is Peloton buying back control of a channel that had been outside their ecosystem entirely.

The secondary market went from revenue leakage → taxed channel → owned platform in approximately 13 months of shareholder letters.

Section 05

How to Read a Subscription Business's Financial Statements

The Peloton story generalizes. Any company with a hardware-plus-subscription model — from fitness equipment to smart home devices to printers — embeds the same analytical structure: hardware drives subscriber acquisition, subscribers drive recurring revenue, and the relationship between those two things is where the real business model lives. Here's the five-step sequence.

Financial Statement Playbook
Reading a Hardware + Subscription Business
Five steps. Applicable to Peloton, or any company where hardware sales are the mechanism for subscriber acquisition rather than the primary profit driver.
01
Separate the Revenue Streams — Never Accept Consolidated
Hardware revenue and subscription revenue belong in separate analytical buckets. Consolidated revenue obscures whether a decline is structural (hardware falling) or existential (subscriptions falling). For Peloton: total revenue fell $1.1B from peak. Subscription revenue grew $149M over the same period. You cannot see that without splitting the lines.
📌 Where: Income statement → segment revenue disclosure · 10-K MD&A revenue section
02
Look at Gross Margin by Segment — Hardware Margin Is the Signal
Subscription margins in hardware-plus-subscription businesses tend to be stable (content, software, and service have high incremental margins). Hardware margin is the variable that tells you about pricing power, supply chain discipline, and inventory management. A hardware company with negative gross margin is permanently subsidizing subscriber acquisition. Peloton's hardware margin went from −27% to +13.6%. That's the turnaround story — not the headline net loss improvement.
📌 Where: Segment gross profit disclosure · Shareholder letters (margins reported quarterly with commentary)
03
Track ARPU Alongside Subscriber Count — They Can Diverge
Subscriber count is the metric management guides to and media reports on. ARPU — average revenue per user — is the metric that actually determines subscription revenue. When subscriber count falls but subscription revenue holds, ARPU is rising. That can reflect pricing power (healthy) or churn among lower-value subscribers (also informative). Divide subscription revenue by average subscribers to get implied ARPU, then track it across four years.
📌 The math: Annual Sub Revenue ÷ Average (beginning + ending paid subs ÷ 2) × 12 = Implied Monthly ARPU
04
Read the Shareholder Letters Sequentially — Language Shifts Reveal Strategy
Management language in shareholder letters evolves faster than strategy disclosures in 10-Ks. A topic first mentioned cautiously, then quantified, then announced as a new initiative traces the arc of a strategic pivot. Peloton mentioned the secondary market defensively, quantified it favorably, taxed it, then built a marketplace around it — all across four consecutive shareholder letters. The 10-K wouldn't show you that arc at all.
📌 Where: Investor relations page → shareholder letters (quarterly, usually filed as 8-Ks alongside earnings)
05
Compare the Letter to the Call — Framing Differences Are Informative
Shareholder letters are edited for public perception. Earnings call transcripts are real-time, under analyst questioning, and harder to polish. When the same announcement gets different language in both — as with Peloton's activation fee — the call usually reveals the actual economic motivation more directly. Reading both gives you the full picture that reading either alone misses.
📌 Where: Shareholder letters (8-K exhibit) + earnings call transcripts (Seeking Alpha, The Motley Fool, or company IR site)
The Full Circle

Every January, the resolution industrial complex cranks back up. Gym memberships spike. Peloton runs ads. Someone in your neighborhood buys a bike at full retail, convinced this is the year. By March, statistically, that bike is in a spare bedroom. By summer, it's on Facebook Marketplace at a 60% discount.

That cycle nearly destroyed Peloton. The pandemic version of it — compressed into two years instead of twelve months — sent hardware revenue off a cliff and left the company holding factories it didn't need and inventory it couldn't sell. Three restructuring plans. Two CEO changes. A stock down 91% from its peak.

And then, quietly, the filings started telling a different story. The people who bought those Facebook Marketplace bikes in March were subscribing. They were sticking around. Their churn was lower than rental subscribers. Peloton started charging them $95 to activate. Then it built its own marketplace to take 30% of every resale. The quitters became the distribution channel.

I bought one of those March bikes. My wife and I ride it almost every day. I pay $44 a month. Peloton earned nothing on the hardware. From a gross profit perspective — 70% subscription margins, zero hardware acquisition cost — they got the better end of that transaction. The person who quit in February funded my setup. I fund their subscription revenue.

The January resolution boom built this company. The March quitters are now helping rebuild it — differently, more sustainably, through a secondary market that Peloton spent three years learning to embrace.

The shareholder letters documented every step of that realization. The footnotes knew before the strategy deck said so. Almost nobody read them. — Clarity Standards Group
