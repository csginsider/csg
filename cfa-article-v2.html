Before I Became a CFA,
I Almost Bought One.

Two things share the abbreviation CFA — a chicken franchise and a financial credential. Reading the franchise's Franchise Disclosure Document cover to cover didn't make me an operator. It sharpened the analytical instincts that eventually earned me the other one.

How This Started

My family eats at Chick-fil-A constantly. With as many kids as I have, the Spicy Deluxe and a large lemonade is less of a meal and more of a survival strategy. At some point the thought crossed my mind the way it crosses the mind of every loyal customer who watches those drive-thru lines wrap around the block: what if I owned one of these?

The entry fee is $10,000. I'd spent more than that on car repairs. The brand is iconic. The product is exceptional. The lines don't lie. On paper, it seemed like the most obvious business decision in America.

So I did what any trained accountant does before a major financial decision. I requested the FDD and read it cover to cover. All 330 pages. Items 1 through 23. The financial statements in Exhibit C. The fee schedule in Item 6. The equity provisions in Item 17.

What I found didn't make me want to own a Chick-fil-A. It made me want to understand what "ownership" actually means in a franchise agreement — and how different that is from what the word implies.

I ended up keeping the accounting career. A few years later I passed the CFA exam — the Chartered Financial Analyst designation. Three letters. And I've thought about the other CFA ever since, because what that FDD reveals about Chick-fil-A's business model is one of the most elegant — and least understood — financial structures in American business. The document that talked me out of becoming a CFA operator is the same document that sharpened the analytical skills that eventually made me a CFA.

Section 01

The Document Nobody Reads

Every year in April, Chick-fil-A files its Franchise Disclosure Document with state regulators across the country. It is legally required. It is publicly available. It contains the company's audited consolidated financial statements — revenue, earnings, the works. And almost nobody reads it.

That's not an accident. Chick-fil-A has no SEC filing obligation. There's no quarterly earnings call. No analyst day. No 10-K sitting on EDGAR. The company is privately held by the Cathy family, and the FDD — a document designed to inform prospective franchise operators — is essentially the only financial disclosure the public ever sees.

For most people, that's fine. For a CPA reading the document the way you'd read a 10-K, it's a revelation. Because the FDD doesn't just tell you whether you should open a Chick-fil-A. It tells you exactly how the entire business works — who owns what, who earns what, and where the extraordinary economics of the best quick-service restaurant in America actually accumulate.

The answer is not where you'd expect.

FDD Item 6 — Other Fees · Verbatim Language
"...you also must pay Chick-fil-A a 'Base Operating Service Fee' of the amount by which fifteen percent of the gross receipts of the Chick-fil-A Restaurant exceeds the Chick-fil-A Restaurant's equipment rental fee... and an 'Additional Operating Service Fee' of fifty percent of the Operator's net receipts for the Chick-fil-A Restaurant."

"Chick-fil-A owns all equipment, furniture, fixtures, and other items... [and] leases or subleases these properties to franchisees under month-to-month lease arrangements with rent subject to a cap of 6 percent of restaurant sales."
Chick-fil-A Operator Program FDD, Item 6 · Filed annually with state regulators · Available publicly

Read those two paragraphs slowly. Fifteen percent of gross sales as a base fee. Fifty percent of net profit as an additional fee. Real estate and equipment owned by Chick-fil-A corporate, leased back to the operator. Everything the operator touches — the building, the fryers, the furniture — belongs to someone else.

And the operator's entry cost for all of this is $10,000.

Section 02

The AUV Story: The Best Store Numbers in Fast Food

Before we get to the fee math, you need to understand the revenue context, because it's genuinely extraordinary. A Chick-fil-A freestanding location averaged $9.3 million in sales in 2024. That's not revenue per location for Chick-fil-A corporate — that's the average annual gross sales flowing through each operator's restaurant.

McDonald's average U.S. unit does about $4 million. Wendy's, Burger King, and Taco Bell are all in the $2 million range. The single worst-performing standalone Chick-fil-A in the 2025 FDD still outperformed the average KFC. The chain is closed every Sunday — roughly 52 days a year — and still generates more per location than almost anyone else in the industry.

These aren't hand-wavy estimates. The extraordinary AUV matters enormously for what comes next, because when you apply the fee structure to a $9.3 million store, the math becomes clarifying very quickly.

The comparison reveals a counterintuitive result. CFA store volumes are more than double McDonald's. And yet the operator's after-tax take is roughly similar — or potentially lower — because such a large share of the economics flows back to corporate through the fee structure. The franchise expert quoted by TheStreet in December 2025 put it plainly: Chick-fil-A "falls short due to the absence of exit value and limited growth potential."

But here's what that framing misses: the CFA operator invested $10,000. The McDonald's operator invested $1.5 to $2.7 million of their own capital. On a return-on-investment basis, the CFA operator is doing extraordinarily well. The question isn't whether the CFA deal is bad. It's whether it's what the word "ownership" implies — and whether the people who sign up understand what they're actually signing.

"Chick-fil-A is a high-paying management contract. McDonald's is a business you own. Both are good deals — they're just different things, and the FDD is the only place where that difference is spelled out in plain English."

Section 04

The Ownership Question: What Item 17 Actually Says

The equity question sits in Item 17 of the FDD — Renewal, Termination, Transfer and Dispute Resolution. It's the section most prospective operators skim past. You shouldn't.

Chick-fil-A's franchise agreement is explicitly personal and non-transferable. "We will only enter into a franchise agreement with an individual" — not a partnership, not a corporation, not an LLC. The operator cannot sell the franchise. They cannot pass it to their children. They cannot even transfer it to a business entity without CFA's explicit consent, full personal guarantee, and continued personal liability. When the agreement ends — on CFA's terms — the operator walks away with whatever they saved from their annual income. The real estate, the equipment, the building, the brand: all back to corporate.

This is not a flaw in the model. It is the model. Chick-fil-A is not selling you a business. It is selecting you to operate one of theirs. The $10,000 isn't a franchise fee in the traditional sense — the FDD itself notes that $5,000 of it is "deemed fully earned and non-refundable upon payment" and the other $5,000 is "an initial payment of working capital." It's closer to an earnest money deposit than a capital investment.

Section 05

The Corporate Story: The Numbers Behind the Numbers

Here is where the FDD becomes genuinely extraordinary as a financial document. Because in Exhibit C, tucked behind 23 items of franchise disclosures, is something almost no one discusses: Chick-fil-A's audited consolidated financial statements.

Not operator earnings. Not systemwide sales. The actual financial results of Chick-fil-A, Inc. — the corporate entity that owns the real estate, the equipment, and collects the fees.

Corporate revenue in 2024 was $9.06 billion — exact to the dollar at $9,062,620,436 per the FDD. That's the fees, the rent, and the company-operated store sales flowing to Chick-fil-A, Inc. directly. On $22.75 billion in systemwide sales, corporate captured nearly 40 cents of every dollar as its own revenue.

Comprehensive earnings — the FDD's equivalent of net income — were $946 million in 2024, down slightly from $1.05 billion in 2023, largely due to higher G&A costs including a $67 million acquisition. The reported net margin looks modest at around 10%. But that framing misleads.

The Accounting Explanation

Why does CFA's reported margin look so much lower than McDonald's?

McDonald's net margin is ~32%. CFA's is ~10%. The difference isn't operational performance — it's accounting structure. CFA owns approximately 3,100 restaurants' worth of real estate and equipment. That asset base generates enormous annual depreciation charges that flow through the income statement and suppress reported earnings. Strip out the non-cash depreciation and CFA's cash generation looks very different — the 2023 net cash flow was reported at approximately $2.2 billion on $7.9 billion of revenue, closer to 28%.

McDonald's, by contrast, sold most of its real estate to franchisees decades ago. It's an asset-light model — franchisees own the buildings, McDonald's collects royalties at ~82% margin. The accounting looks cleaner because the heavy capital expenditures happened in the franchisees' balance sheets, not McDonald's.

CFA is simultaneously a restaurant company and a commercial real estate company — and the income statement blends both. The real estate portfolio is worth billions. It just doesn't show up as income — it shows up as depreciation.

Section 06

A New Development: 425 More Restaurants, Same Model

In December 2025, TheStreet reported something that hadn't gotten nearly enough attention: Chick-fil-A is actively converting its 425 licensed locations — the restaurants in college cafeterias, hospital lobbies, airport terminals — to the full operator model. Right now. As you read this.

Those 425 locations currently operate under a simpler licensing arrangement. Under the full operator program, they would come under the same fee structure detailed in Items 6 and 17: the 15% base fee, the 50% profit share, the CFA-owned equipment, the personal and non-transferable franchise agreement. The same model that generates $9 billion in corporate revenue from 3,100 locations gets extended to 425 more.

Chick-fil-A is not just maintaining this model. It's expanding it. The 330-page document filed every April tells you exactly why.

Financial Statement Playbook
How to Read a Franchise Disclosure Document
Five items. Works for any franchise, not just Chick-fil-A. The FDD is the document that replaces the 10-K for private franchise companies — and most people never read it.
01
Item 6 — Other Fees: Find the Real Cost of Operating
The entry fee (Item 5) is the number everyone quotes. Item 6 is the number that actually matters — the ongoing fees as a percentage of revenue. For CFA: 15% base + 50% of net profit. For McDonald's: 4–5% royalty + 4% advertising. The fee structure determines how economics are divided between operator and franchisor across the entire life of the franchise.
📌 Look for: royalty %, advertising %, any profit-sharing language, equipment/rent charges payable to franchisor
02
Item 7 — Estimated Initial Investment: Who's Really Funding This?
Compare the total investment range (Item 7) to what the operator pays versus what the franchisor funds. CFA's total investment is $444K–$2.9M per location — but the operator pays $10,000 of that. The gap is what CFA fronts and recovers through fees over time. McDonald's total investment ($1.47M–$2.73M) comes almost entirely from the operator. The funding structure tells you everything about who bears the risk.
📌 Look for: total investment range vs. franchise fee paid directly to franchisor — the gap is what the operator must fund or what corporate subsidizes
03
Item 17 — Transfer, Termination, Renewal: The Equity Question
This is where you find out whether you actually own something. Can you sell it? Transfer it to heirs? Renew on the same terms? CFA's Item 17 is explicit: the agreement is personal, non-transferable, cannot be passed to children, and cannot be held by a corporate entity without full personal guarantee. McDonald's operators can sell their franchise — typically at 3–5x earnings — and build transferable wealth. Read Item 17 before you read anything else.
📌 Look for: transfer provisions, right of first refusal, whether franchisor must approve sale, whether sale is even permitted
04
Item 19 — Financial Performance Representations: The Unit Economics
Not all franchisors disclose Item 19 (they're not required to). When they do, it's the closest thing to per-unit P&L data you'll find. For CFA: average AUV of $9.3M for non-mall locations, with distribution data (33rd percentile, median, 66th percentile). Compare median to high — if the median is much lower than the average, the distribution is right-skewed by a few exceptional stores. For CFA, median and average are close — a healthy sign.
📌 Look for: average vs. median (skewness), sample size vs. total units (selection bias), what's excluded from the sample
05
Exhibit C — Financial Statements: The Franchisor's Own Economics
This is the section almost nobody reads. Franchisors are required to include audited financial statements in their FDD. For CFA, Exhibit C contains the full consolidated financials — revenue, earnings, asset base. For a private company like CFA, this is the only financial disclosure they make. The $9.06B corporate revenue, the $946M comprehensive earnings, the relationship between systemwide sales and corporate capture — it's all in Exhibit C, one time per year, available to anyone who asks for the FDD.
📌 Look for: revenue breakdown (franchised fees vs. company sales), net income trend, asset structure (heavy vs. light capital model)
What the FDD Actually Shows

I still eat at Chick-fil-A constantly. The Spicy Deluxe is exceptional, the service is impeccable, and those drive-thru lines don't lie. The product earns every bit of the loyalty it commands. And in full disclosure: reading the FDD didn't talk me out of respecting the business. It deepened it.

But I understand the business differently now than I did before I read that FDD. What looks like a franchise — the small entry fee, the operator model, the "ownership" language — is actually something more precise and more interesting. Chick-fil-A has built a model where they fund the asset, hire a highly qualified manager to run it, pay that manager very well, and keep the business. The $10,000 entry fee is not the price of a franchise. It's the price of selection.

The corporate economics that flow from that structure are extraordinary — $9 billion in revenue from 3,100 locations, captured by a private family-owned company that discloses its financials exactly once a year in a document designed for prospective operators. McDonald's is analyzed by 40 Wall Street analysts every quarter. Chick-fil-A files an FDD in April and almost nobody reads Exhibit C.

I didn't open a CFA. I became one instead. The analytical skills are the same either way — read the document, understand the fee structure, follow the cash. The FDD is where the real story lives. The other CFA will always be the one that got me here. — Clarity Standards Group
